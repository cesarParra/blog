This morning while sipping my coffee and watching the Cubs highlights I realized there are a couple of things that I haven't experienced in a long time: being in a "flow" state and having an Eureka moment.

Not experiencing a "flow" state is easily explained. I have (we all have) been mostly letting AI do the clickety-clack part of our work. Our modern agentic way of working is not conducive to achieving flow. We are encouraged to run a million agents in parallel, context switching through a dozen tasks. There's never any time to get into a groove. Letting the code flow through my fingers was what (personally) made me reach flow state, not reviewing the output (especially one that I didn't write).

But not having an Eureka moment in a while is more puzzling. I feel like I used to have one at least once every couple of weeks. But it has been months, maybe over a year?

If you don't know, an Eureka moment is the moment where, after struggling with a difficult problem without making any headway for a while, you suddenly get the answer when not even thinking about it. It happens while walking, driving, taking a shower, or just going about your business after you finally stop stressing about the issue. These are the most gratifying moments you can have as a thought worker.

So why haven't I experienced this in a while? You'd think that with the increased productivity that AI allows I'm now working on so many more interesting things, unlocking in me the ability to tackle so more many problems that I either didn't have the time or the knowledge to tackle before. There should be so many more opportunities for ideas to pop! 

Some of this is true, I am working on things I previously wouldn't even dare. But there's one important piece of the puzzle that's missing: friction. 

Friction can be a good thing. It is a signal. It let's you know there's something you don't know, something you can't (yet) do, something (or someone) you disagree with.

The good kind of friction encourages action. It makes you try and learn something new, resolve a disagreement, reach a compromise, make a decision. This leads to deeper knowledge and understanding. Solving friction leads to having an emotional connection with your work.

This lack of friction is what is leading to my lack of Eureka moments. There's no friction to overcome, thus no "aha!" moments to be had. I am not reaching out for deeper understanding, thus there's nothing being learned. I'm not emotionally connecting with the work, so my subconscious is not bothering working overtime on the issue.

This might feel like a rant. Maybe it is. I'm mostly just nostalgic.

If it is a rant its not against AI.

AI doesn't take away my ability to focus and stop context switching. It doesn't take away my ability to solve problems, seek understanding, care deeply about what I do. What it does is tempt you. Having the power of a God with the accumulated knowledge of humanity at your fingertips tempts me to let it think for me, figure things out for me. It tempts me to let an Agent run wild, one-shot the full solution, come back to me with an answer. But at the end of the day the power I give it (or don't) is fully up to me.

And maybe that's the key takeaway here, not that I'm looking for one. This thing shouldn't be thinking for me.  It shouldn't make decisions by its own, it should stop and ask me. Maybe that takes away some of the "speed" it allows us to reach, but I prefer to move steadily and consistently, with a deep understanding of what is going on, than moving fast and breaking things. There's a reason that [thinking is fast and slow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thinking%2C_Fast_and_Slow).

These days I've had the opportunity to come back to an organization and work on a codebase that I hadn't touched in several years. Oftentimes I open up a file and I instantly recognize it as something that I wrote. Yes, it carries my "style" which is easy for me to recognize, but the codebase also left a mark in me. Not because I remember each keystroke as I typed them, or the IDE I typed them in. I recognize it because it left an emotional impact. Behind every line of code there was a decision made, and another dozen not made. There was a disagreement during code review, a conversation with a Product Owner. Dozens of Eureka moments.

The thinking, the decision making, the final say, those are the things that we should not, cannot, let ourselves outsource to the magic thinking box. At least if you enjoy the craft, that is.
